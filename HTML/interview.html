Interview with Head of Humanities (Shannon Simpson)

Q1: Who are you and what is your role within the school?

Mr Simpson, Head of Humanities Faculty, began teaching at Rostrevor in 2009.

Q2: Are you aware of Robbie’s corner? If so, what is your connection with the location?

Yes, I’ve become aware of Robbie’s Corner in recent years, given it is on a path I and others walk past the Boarding House, and along the College’s Kaurna Trail. Robbie was a former Rostrevor student, who sadly passed away during 2000. Mr Simon Kelly (former Rostrevor College Head of Studies of Society and Environment, 1992-2002), plus other staff and students, attended Robbie’s funeral at Point Pearce. Later, the memorial was created outside the Boarding House, with a tree planted in memory of Robbie, and an accompanying plaque.

Q3: Has the lack of the school’s acknowledgement of Robbie’s Corner hindered its impact on the school?

Possibly; the school has other memorials of this type, and I believe it’s important for the College community to be aware of the stories of past staff and students that these memorials tell.

Q4: What could we as the Rostrevor community give Robbie's Corner More Significance?

Robbie’s Corner, and other similar sites around the College, could be added to the walk/tour that year 7s engage with during their Enculturation Day. And/or, Robbie’s Corner should become more clearly sign-posted or labelled on a map of the College’s Kaurna Trail.

Q5: To what extent should the school be recognising Robbie’s Corner?

As above – the memorial site should be maintained by staff and/or students, so, kept neat and tidy, and the stories then of Robbie and others can be better captured and shared by the community.



Interview with a Current Boarding Student (Kade Maddigan)

Q1: Who are you and what is your connection to the school?

I am Kade Maddigan, and I am a boarder in year 12. I commenced my journey as a year 9 student in 2020 enjoying the change in environment from a country town. My connection to the school is just being a good human and offering my services to make the school and wider community a better place. I like to spread as much positivity as possible and enjoy the connections built in a boarding environment.

Q2: Are you aware of Robbie’s corner? If so, what is your connection with the location?

I was not aware of Robbie’s corner prior to the commencement of this subject and I think that it is under recognized as a community.

Q3: Has the lack of the school’s acknowledgement of Robbie’s Corner hindered its impact on the school?

As previously mentioned I did not know that Robbie’s Corner was a plaque in the school which is a bit disappointing. And yes I do think that Robbie’s impact on the school has not been acknowledged enough as it should have been.

Q4: What could we as the Rostrevor community give Robbie's Corner More Significance?

I think that clearing the area around it would make a big impact as it is quite hard to see and it isn’t really a place where people can go to connect to their spirituality or if they are having a hard time.

Q5: To what extent should the school be recognising Robbie’s Corner?

They should be recognizing it a lot more as he was an important figure in the Rostrevor community and his life is not made aware of enough as it should have.

Interview with an Ex-Rostrevor Teacher (Simon Kelly)

General Notes from the Interview

  • 1999-2007 Indigenous Coordinator

  • Very close with Robbie. Came from Port Pierce, Noarunga territory.
  • Starte in Year 8, Kelly started knowing him in Year 10. 1999 when he got to know him best.
  • Bernie Dean Boarding House master at the time.
  • A number of boys missed out on sports and so Bernie set up a special boarding team, Robbie was keen on cricket
  • Simon’s son had a frienship with Robbie, Matthew. Primary school son, has a great relationship with Robbie.
  • Half past 4 Robbie is heading out of school when students need to be back by 5 at the boarding house. He was a bit of a cheeky student.
  • Robbie loved cricket.
  • The vehicle he was in was run off the road. 4 in the crash, 2 passed away.
  • The corner he sat on has now become his corner.
  • Half way through Year 11 Robbie decided he needed a break. There was always a place at Rostrevor for Robbie. Sadly, in this break he came into a car crash. He died in this crash.
  • Robbie would sit on the rock all of the time to think about things. The tree over it was planted after his death.
  • Robbie’s funeral was at Port Pierce, a minibus of people, teachers, school leaders and students in the boarding house.
  • Robbie was buried in the school uniform, the funeral happened in a small chapel in port pierce.
  • Bo Bo, Robbie’s uncle.
  • The funeral procession had, the hurse with the casket, all of the Rossi boys, and then the family. That’s how important the school was in his life.
  • Simon Kely dropped a new cricket ball into the coffin as a part of his life.
  • Sometime after his funeral, Bearnie Dean gathered everyone around in a circle. Louie and Debbie, Robbie’s parents came along. They started talking first and everyone had a conversation about remembering Robbie.
  • Everyone, especially the female relatives, were wailing and crying during the funeral ceremonies.
  • Robbie was a good kid at heart.
  • It was considered a privellege to be there at Port Pierce, and being placed before the family just proves how much Rostrevor meant to his family and the Port Pierce community.
